About Sigmund Freud<

Sigmund Freud (May 6, 1856 â€“ September 23, 1939) was an Austrian psychiatrist, psychologist, and the founder of the psychoanalytic school. In 1873, he enrolled in the Medical School of the University of Vienna, earning his medical doctorate in 1881. From 1882 to 1885, he served as a physician at the Vienna General Hospital, conducting research on brain anatomy and pathology. He later opened a private practice specializing in the treatment of mental illnesses. In 1895, he formally introduced the concept of psychoanalysis. His book The Interpretation of Dreams, published in 1899, is considered the formal establishment of psychoanalytic psychology. In 1919, the International Psychoanalytical Association was founded, marking the final formation of the psychoanalytic school. In 1930, he was awarded the Goethe Prize. In 1936, he became a member of the British Royal Society. In 1938, after Austria was annexed by Germany, he fled to the United Kingdom and passed away in London the following year. Freud pioneered the study of the unconscious mind, advanced the development of dynamic psychology, personality psychology, and abnormal psychology, laid a new foundation for the modern medical model, and provided an important theoretical pillar for Western humanities in the 20th century.
